## Quick Summary

Two dads — one educated, conventional, employee-minded (the "poor dad") and one entrepreneurial, investor-minded (the "rich dad") — give the author conflicting advice about money. The book contrasts these philosophies and argues for an asset-first approach to building wealth.

## Key Takeaways

### 1. The rich don't work for money
The central insight: employees work for money, but money works for the rich. The goal is to build assets that generate income without requiring your direct labor.

### 2. The difference between an asset and a liability
An asset puts money in your pocket. A liability takes money out of your pocket. Most people confuse the two — a house is not an asset if it costs you mortgage, taxes, and maintenance without generating income.

### 3. Mind your own business
Your job is not your business. Your business is what you own — the assets on your balance sheet. Keep your day job, but build your business on the side.

### 4. The rich invest in assets
Stocks, bonds, real estate, intellectual property, businesses that generate income. The wealthy continuously convert income into these assets.

### 5. Work to learn, not just to earn
Kiyosaki emphasizes acquiring skills over maximizing salary. Sales, marketing, accounting, management — these skills transfer across industries and opportunities.

## Favorite Quotes

> "The single most powerful asset we all have is our mind."

> "It's not how much money you make. It's how much money you keep."

## How It Changed My Thinking

This book made me reframe how I think about income. I started separating the money that comes in from my job (trading time for money) from the money that could come in from assets (money working for me).

I started investing. I started a side project. I began tracking my assets vs. liabilities.

Not everything in the book aged well — Kiyosaki's investment advice has been criticized, and some claims are exaggerated. But the mindset shift was valuable regardless.

## Who Should Read This

Anyone early in their financial journey who hasn't thought much about assets, liabilities, and passive income. It's not a how-to guide — it's a philosophy primer.
