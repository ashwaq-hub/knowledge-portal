Dear you,

I know it hurts. That feeling in your chest, the tightness, the replaying of what you could have done differently — I know it well. I've been there.

Here's what I want you to know:

## Rejection is not a verdict on your worth

When a company says no, a school says no, someone says no — they're making a judgment about a specific application, at a specific moment, given specific alternatives. They're not evaluating your value as a human being.

It feels like they are. It feels personal. But it's not.

## Every person you admire has been rejected more than you

The writers whose work you love got rejected. The entrepreneurs you admire heard "no" hundreds of times. The artists you respect were told they weren't good enough. The only difference between them and people who gave up is that they kept going.

## "No" often means "not right now"

I've been rejected from jobs I later realized would have been wrong for me. I've been turned down for opportunities that redirected me toward something better. Rejection often closes a door you would have walked through and found frustration on the other side.

You don't know that yet. You will.

## What to do with rejection

1. Feel it. Don't suppress it. Disappointment is real and deserves acknowledgment.

2. Learn what you can. Was there something in your control? Did you rush the application? Could you have prepared better? Learn without catastrophizing.

3. Move on. Apply to the next one. Start the next project. Build the next thing. Momentum is the antidote to rejection.

## The secret

Rejection gets easier. Not because you become immune, but because you accumulate evidence that you survive it. Each rejection you recover from is proof that you can handle the next one.

You're going to be fine.

With love,
[Your name]
