## The Problem with Budgets

Every budget I've ever tried has failed. Not because I'm bad with money — I'm an engineer by training — but because traditional budgets are:

1. **Restrictive.** They feel like deprivation.
2. **Time-intensive.** They require constant tracking.
3. **All-or-nothing.** One slip-up derails the whole system.

The typical response: abandon the budget, feel guilty, repeat.

## What Actually Changed

A few years ago, I stumbled on a different approach. It's not flashy. It's not new. But it works.

### Step 1: Pay Yourself First

Before anything else — before rent, before bills, before discretionary spending — set aside savings.

I started at 10%. When that became comfortable, I increased to 15%. Then 20%. Now 25%.

The key: this happens automatically. The savings come out the day I get paid, before I have a chance to spend it.

### Step 2: Name Your Goals

Savings for its own sake is abstract. Savings tied to a goal is motivating.

I have separate accounts for:
- Emergency fund (6 months of expenses)
- Vacation (one trip per year)
- New laptop fund
- Early retirement fund

When I see the number growing, I see progress toward something real.

### Step 3: The One Rule

This is the part that made it stick:

**You can spend anything you want from what's left after savings. No tracking. No guilt.**

I removed all the tracking apps. Deleted the spreadsheets. Stopped categorizing every coffee purchase.

The constraint is upstream: savings comes out first. What's left is mine to spend freely.

## What This Changed

- My savings rate went from ~5% to 25% within a year
- I stopped the guilt-spending cycle
- I became more intentional about big purchases (I want them to fit in "what's left")
- Money stress dropped dramatically

## The Underlying Insight

Most budgeting fails because it treats spending as the enemy. This approach treats spending as neutral — you earned it, you can spend it. The enemy is unallocated money that disappears into nothing.

Pay yourself first. The rest takes care of itself.
