## The Situation

It was 2018. I had just received my first job offer after graduate school — a role at a consulting firm I genuinely wanted to join. The offer was $75,000. I was thrilled. I had student loans, barely any savings, and this felt like a fortune.

They asked: "Is this number acceptable to you?"

I said yes. Immediately. Without hesitation. Without negotiation.

What I didn't know: the budget for the role was $85,000-$95,000. They had offered me the bottom of the range, partly because I didn't ask for more, partly because they could.

## What I Did

Nothing. I said "yes, that's great, thank you." I was so afraid of losing the offer that I treated the first number as the only number.

I signed the paperwork within 24 hours.

## What Happened

Six months later, I found out a colleague — same role, same experience level — was making $12,000 more than me. She had negotiated. She had asked.

The work was identical. The performance reviews were identical. The difference between us was one conversation I was too afraid to have.

Over three years at that company, that gap compounded. Her raises built on a higher base. Her bonus was higher. Her equity grant was larger. By the time I left, the cumulative difference was over $50,000.

## What I Learned

**The cost of not asking is always higher than the risk of asking.**

When you receive an offer, it's the opening position in a negotiation — not the final answer. Companies expect negotiation. HR is trained for it. When you don't negotiate, you signal one of two things: either you're desperate, or you don't understand your own worth. Neither is what you want to communicate.

The worst case of asking is they say no. That's it. You lose nothing except the silence you were already living in.

## What I'd Tell You

Before any negotiation — job offer, salary review, contract, big purchase — do this:

1. **Know your number.** What's the minimum you'll accept? What's the ideal outcome? What's your walk-away point?
2. **Research.** What does the market pay? What did the last person in this role get? Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, blind outreach.
3. **Practice the ask.** Out loud. In front of a mirror or a friend. Friction kills negotiation.
4. **Ask for more than you expect.** You won't get everything you ask for. Aim for the range, not the floor.
5. **Always, always ask.** "Is there flexibility in this number?" — four words that could change your career trajectory.

## When This Applies

**When it applies:** Job offers, salary reviews, freelance rates, contract terms, big purchases (cars, houses), service agreements.

**When it doesn't:** Fixed-price situations, transparent auctions, when asking would genuinely damage a relationship you're invested in long-term.

*The core lesson: Silence has a price. Ask.*
