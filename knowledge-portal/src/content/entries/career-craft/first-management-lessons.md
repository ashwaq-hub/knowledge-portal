## The Surprise

I thought being a manager meant having more influence, better decisions, and eventually better pay. I was right about all three.

I was wrong about everything else.

## What I Didn't Expect

### 1. Your success is now mediated through others

As an individual contributor (IC), your output is your output. You write the code, you close the deal, you do the work.

As a manager, your output is your team's output. You can be brilliant and if your team fails, you fail. This is a hard adjustment. It felt like losing control. It was actually gaining leverage.

### 2. Feedback is your most important job

Not feedback as criticism — feedback as information flow. Tell people what they're doing well. Tell them what they're not doing well. Do it frequently, not annually.

The best managers I've had were relentlessly candid. The worst were the ones who avoided difficult conversations.

### 3. You will lose your technical edge

This is fine. It's also scary.

As a manager, you spend more time on strategy, hiring, conflict, communication, and prioritization. You spend less time deep in the technical details. That's the trade.

The fear is that you'll become irrelevant. The answer is: your relevance changes. You go from being the best at doing the work to being the best at making sure the work gets done.

### 4. Meetings will consume your calendar

Before management, I hated meetings. After management, I understood: meetings are how work gets coordinated, decisions get made, and relationships get built.

Not all meetings are valuable. But most are more valuable than I used to think.

### 5. The loneliest job in the company

Managers often can't share problems upward (it looks like failure) or sideways (it's not their problem). You carry things alone.

Build a peer network of other managers. Find mentors. Have a friend outside your company who understands what you're going through.

## What I'd Tell New Managers

- **Hire slowly, fire quickly.** Bad hires cost more than vacant positions.
- **One-on-ones are sacred.** Never cancel them. They are the most important meeting on your calendar.
- **Say no to your boss regularly.** Your job is to push back, not just say yes.
- **Promote for trajectory, not for tenure.** Who someone is becoming matters more than who they've been.
- **Make decisions with incomplete information.** Waiting for certainty is often the wrong call.

## The Adjustment Period

It took me about 18 months to feel competent again. The learning curve is real. Be patient with yourself.

The best managers I've known made peace with the fact that management is a different skill set, not a better version of IC work. They stopped comparing themselves to how they used to perform and started focusing on how their teams were performing.

The shift isn't easy. It's worth it.
