## The Situation

I spent years thinking a good marriage was about finding the right person — compatibility, chemistry, shared interests. Like a lock-and-key problem. Find the right fit, and the rest follows.

What I didn't understand: marriage is not a state you achieve. It's a practice you maintain. Every day.

## What I Did

For the first few years, I coasted on the chemistry that brought us together. I assumed that because we loved each other, the relationship would be fine. I didn't invest in it the way I invested in my career or my health.

I was wrong. And the relationship showed it.

## What Happened

We hit a rough patch — not a dramatic crisis, just a slow drift. Less connection. More distance. Conversations that stayed on the surface.

The turning point was a question she asked: "When did you stop being curious about me?"

I didn't have an answer. But the question was right.

## What I Learned

**Sustained marriages run on four things — not chemistry:**

### 1. Curiosity, not assumptions
You stop growing together the moment you think you already know everything about your partner. People change. Ask questions. Stay interested. The alternative is living with a stranger who looks familiar.

### 2. Repair, not avoidance
Every couple argues. What separates the lasting ones: how fast they repair. Letting conflict sit hardens it. A short "I was out of line earlier" does more work than you think.

### 3. Bids for connection
Research by John Gottman shows that couples make constant small "bids" for connection — a comment, a touch, a joke. Partners who last turn toward these bids. Those who drift turn away — often without realizing it.

### 4. Separate identities
Losing yourself in a relationship doesn't strengthen it. Two people who stay whole — with their own friendships, interests, and inner lives — bring more to each other than two people who become one fused unit.

## What I'd Tell You

- Choose someone whose character you respect, not just someone who makes you feel good right now. Feelings fluctuate. Character is what you live with.
- Marriage will reveal every unresolved thing about you. That's not a flaw in the institution — that's what it's for.
- The "work" in relationships is not dramatic — it's small, daily, unglamorous acts of attention and repair.
- Never outsource your emotional life entirely to one person. That's not partnership; it's dependency. And it will crush them and you.
- Say the thing you're about to swallow. Most resentment is built from things unsaid.

## When This Applies

**When it applies:** Long-term romantic partnerships, marriage, committed relationships.

**When it doesn't:** Early-stage dating (different rules apply — this is about sustaining, not starting).
