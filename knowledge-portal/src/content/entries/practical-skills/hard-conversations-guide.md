## Overview

Hard conversations are the ones we avoid. The feedback we don't give. The boundary we don't set. The concern we don't raise. The conflict we let simmer.

Avoiding hard conversations is easier in the short term. It's almost always worse in the long term.

This is the framework I use when I can't avoid it anymore.

## What You'll Need

- A genuine desire to resolve the issue, not just vent
- Willingness to hear something uncomfortable
- Basic emotional regulation (yours first)

## Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Get clear on what you actually want

Before the conversation, ask yourself: what outcome am I hoping for?

Not "I want them to stop" — that's a complaint. What do you actually want? Changed behavior? An apology? Understanding? A decision?

If you can't articulate a desired outcome, you're not ready to have the conversation.

### Step 2: Choose the right time and place

Don't ambush someone in public. Don't raise a sensitive topic when either of you is tired, hungry, or stressed.

Ask: "Can we talk about X? I want to discuss [specific topic]. When works for you?"

### Step 3: Start with shared context

Begin with what you both agree on. The issue exists within a relationship that presumably has value to both of you.

Example: "I value our working relationship, and I want to talk about something that's been bothering me. Is now a good time?"

### Step 4: Describe the behavior, not the person

"I noticed that..." / "When you..." / "The pattern I'm seeing..."

Avoid: "You always..." / "You never..." / "You're the problem..."

The goal is to describe observable behavior, not assign character.

### Step 5: Explain the impact

"The result of this is..." / "What happens when this happens is..."

Make clear why this matters to you. Connect it to the impact on work, the relationship, the team.

### Step 6: Listen

Shut up and listen. Let them respond. Let them explain.

This is the step most people skip because they're so focused on making their point that they don't hear the other person.

### Step 7: Problem-solve together

If you're having a hard conversation, the goal is usually resolution — not winning. Ask: "How do you think we should handle this?"

Work toward something you can both live with.

## Common Mistakes

- **Starting with "I need to talk to you about something"** — vague warnings create anxiety. Be specific.
- **Using "but" to negate everything before it** — "I appreciate you, BUT..." erases the appreciation.
- **Having the conversation when you're angry** — wait until you can speak clearly.
- **Surprising someone in a meeting or public setting** — private for sensitive, public only for celebrations.

## Pro Tips

- Write down what you want to say first. Practice.
- If you've been avoiding the conversation for weeks, that's a sign it needs to happen.
- Assume positive intent. Most people aren't trying to hurt you.

## When to Ask for Help

If the conversation is about serious conflict, harassment, or something beyond your expertise, involve a mediator, HR, or a manager. You don't have to handle everything alone.
