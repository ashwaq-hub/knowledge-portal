## The Problem with Big Decisions

Most major decisions fail not because we choose wrong, but because we overcomplicate them. We build elaborate pro/con lists, run spreadsheets, consult everyone we know, and end up more confused than when we started.

Or we do the opposite: we decide with our gut, act impulsively, and wonder later why things fell apart.

I've tried both. Neither works consistently.

## The Framework: Four Questions

After years of reflection — and several expensive mistakes — I developed a simple framework. I use it for career moves, financial decisions, relationship choices, and anything that keeps me up at night.

### Question 1: What does the "future me" think?

Imagine it's five years from now. You made this choice. How do you feel?

Not the hypothetical best-case future you — the realistic one. The one who has to live with the consequences, handle the Tuesday afternoon frustrations, explain this to their spouse at dinner.

This question cuts through the fantasy and forces honest assessment.

### Question 2: What am I optimizing for?

Every decision involves tradeoffs. Speed vs. quality. Safety vs. growth. Short-term comfort vs. long-term opportunity.

Before choosing, name what you're actually optimizing for. Usually, it's one of:
- Security (paying bills, stability)
- Growth (learning, challenge, skill)
- Impact (meaning, legacy, contribution)
- Freedom (time, flexibility, autonomy)

You rarely get to maximize all four. Knowing which one matters most right now changes the calculus.

### Question 3: What's the worst-case scenario, and can I survive it?

Not "could it happen" — that's anxiety. The question is: **if the worst happened, could I recover?**

Most worst-case scenarios are survivable. We catastrophize because our brains are wired for threat detection, not accurate risk assessment.

If the worst is survivable and recoverable, the risk is usually acceptable.

### Question 4: Will I regret not trying?

This is the question that has guided me most consistently. Not "will I regret trying and failing?" — usually the answer is no. Failing at something you tried is almost always better than the slow burn of wondering "what if?"

The regrets that haunt people longest are the ones they never attempted.

## How to Use This

I write these four questions down. Each in a sentence or two. I don't overthink the answers — I write the first thing that feels true.

The pattern usually becomes clear quickly.

## When This Doesn't Work

This framework works for decisions with real stakes — career, relationships, major purchases, life direction.

It doesn't work for low-stakes daily choices (what to eat, what to watch). Don't apply a heavyweight framework to lightweight decisions.

It also doesn't work if you're not honest with yourself. The framework reveals what you already know but might not want to face.
