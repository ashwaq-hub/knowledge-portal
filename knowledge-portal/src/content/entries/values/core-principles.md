## Why Write Down Principles?

Principles are easy to hold when things are going well. They're tested when you're under pressure, when there's a shortcut available, when compromise seems rational.

I started writing mine down after several situations where I made choices that felt wrong in the moment but I did anyway because I hadn't been clear with myself about what I actually believed.

These are not aspirational. They're tested. I've broken each one at least once and paid for it.

## The Seven

### 1. Tell the truth, even when it costs you

The cost of a lie is always higher than the cost of honesty — even when the truth is painful. Lies compound. Truths simplify.

### 2. Do what you say you'll do

Your word is your currency. If you say you'll call, call. If you say you'll show up, show up. Reliability is rare. Be known for it.

### 3. Make decisions with incomplete information

Waiting for perfect information means waiting forever. Make the best decision you can with what you have, then adapt. Movement beats paralysis.

### 4. Protect your attention

Your attention determines what you think about, what you create, what you notice. Guard it fiercely. Treat distraction as the enemy it is.

### 5. Say no to almost everything

Every yes is a no to something else. Most good things in life require sustained focus. You can't sustain focus if you're spread across twenty commitments.

### 6. Invest in things that compound

Time, money, relationships, skills — these all compound. One hour of focused learning compounds into capability. One dollar invested compounds into wealth. Prioritize things that grow.

### 7. Assume positive intent

Most people are not trying to hurt you. Most conflicts are based on misunderstanding, not malice. Start from positive intent, and you'll be right most of the time. When you're wrong, you'll find out faster than if you started hostile.

## When These Get Tested

These principles conflict with each other. Sometimes you can't protect your attention AND say yes to something meaningful. Sometimes telling the truth hurts someone you care about.

Principles aren't rules. They're guides. The value is in knowing what you believe before the situation forces a split-second decision.
